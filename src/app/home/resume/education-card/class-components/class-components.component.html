Dominos

The stand out assignments from this class have got to be Dominos part 1 and 2. The main arc of them could be simplified into one question: How do we comply with a client's representation of a concept that we developed, and use it with our representatation of the same concept? For example, at an instance level, our domino might be represented as two integer variables, aptly named lowPipCount and highPipCount. But say the method in which our client interfaces with our instance is a string where the first number is the high, the second the low and delimited by a colon. This translation can also be represented in a table as so:

Client Internal
highLowString = "0:0" lowPipCount = 0, highPipCount = 0
highLowString = "1:0" lowPipCount = 0, highPipCount = 1
highLowString = "2:0" lowPipCount = 0, highPipCount = 2
Client Internal
highLowString = "3:0" lowPipCount = 0, highPipCount = 3
highLowString = "4:0" lowPipCount = 0, highPipCount = 4
highLowString = "5:0" lowPipCount = 0, highPipCount = 5

so on and so forth. With this in mind, how do we as the developrs of the program/interface contend with this mismatch is concept representation? In essence that's what Dominos was; lots different ways of representing the same concept interfacing and translating between different client facing representations of the dominos concept.

Ambiguous Ambiguity

Though that does seem to be the assignment that many alumni of the class remember, the heart of this class, I believe, was in all of its weirdness and how it leans on ambiguity. Assignment handouts were often times left short on information, documented unclearly or in an unhelpful way and once asked for the incorrect formatting on deliverables. Intentionally. This was especially terrifying because you had one submission and no ones tests to run against except if you had made your own.

Some students found it frustrating which I can understand, but like no other class before it, it was designed to reward those who took the time to read the assignment sheets early and to spending time looking for potential errors, inconsistencies and/or short comings. If you didn't, the difference between getting a 50 and a 100 might as well have been a coin flip. Like no other class before it makes you appreciate good documentation and well-formed specifications in documentation. But, though perhaps more due to the nature of the assignment concepts, it really forced you to come up with good clarifying questions and to find ways to clearly articulate your point.

The assignments we had in combination with the professor, Components is easily one of my favourite classes in university and comes to be one closets we get to learning about design principles and the limitations of that design outside our Software Engineering class.
